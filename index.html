Abstract

Neural radiance fields (NeRF) have been widely studied for novel view synthesis. However, NeRF has a well-known limitation in that it only shows promising performance if a massive amount of multi-view images taken under consistent illuminations are provided. Reducing the number of required inputs and loosening the restricted lighting assumption are both necessarily needed for the practical usage of NeRF. In this paper, we take a step further and design ExtremeNeRF to synthesize a novel view image in an extremely practical environment where only a small number of multi-view images taken under varying illuminations are available. Specifically, by extracting intrinsic image components that should be shared among different views, our method enables direct color comparison between the input and novel view instead of interpolating colors between views like NeRF does. An additional depth consistency loss that enforces correct geometry synthesis is also proposed. Our framework is evaluated with illumination-varying multi-view datasets including a newly built NeRF Extreme benchmark, which is a first in-the-wild NeRF benchmark that has both multiple viewing directions and varying-illuminations.
